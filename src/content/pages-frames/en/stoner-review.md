*Stoner* was published in 1965, went out of print, was rediscovered in Europe in the 2000s, and has been quietly changing people ever since. It is, on its surface, the story of a man who goes to the University of Missouri to study agriculture, discovers literature, becomes an English professor, and lives and dies in relative obscurity.

Nothing dramatic happens. And yet.

## Why It Works

John Williams writes about ordinary failure with a tenderness and precision that is, paradoxically, exhilarating. William Stoner does not change the world. He has a difficult marriage, a difficult career, one great love affair that ends. He teaches students who don't always appreciate him. He grows old.

But Williams makes you understand that this life — modest, constrained, unrecognized — contained real dignity and real beauty. The love Stoner has for literature is itself a kind of love story, and it runs through everything.

## The Famous Paragraph

There is a passage near the end of the book where Stoner picks up one of his old publications and reflects on it. I have read it four times and I cannot get through it without something happening in my chest.

I won't quote it here. Find it yourself. It's worth it.

**Rating: 5/5** — I think about this book at least once a week.
