There's a particular kind of light that exists only in Lisbon. It arrives around five in the afternoon, slanting gold across the white azulejo tiles and turning the Tagus into hammered bronze. I've chased similar light in Seville and Porto, but Lisbon holds it differently — longer, warmer, as if the city knows something about time that other cities have forgotten.

## Alfama at Dawn

I arrived before the tour groups, which meant waking at six and stumbling uphill through streets so steep they require handrails. The reward was Alfama in silence, just the distant clang of tram number 28 and the smell of bread baking somewhere below.

Miradouro das Portas do Sol was completely empty. I sat on the low wall and watched the city wake up slowly, chimneys breathing, laundry appearing floor by floor.

## On Pastéis de Nata

The correct place to eat pastéis de nata is standing up, at the counter, with a small espresso. Not at a table. Not from a box. The pastry should still be warm enough that the custard trembles when you pick it up. A dusting of cinnamon, no more.

**Pastéis de Belém** is the famous one, and it earns the queue. But I found equal pleasure at a nameless counter café near the Mouraria, where the owner refilled my espresso without asking and we communicated entirely through nods.

## Fado in the Evening

Fado isn't sad, exactly. It's *saudade* — a Portuguese word that resists translation. Longing for something that may never have existed. I heard it properly for the first time in a restaurant with eight tables and a singer who closed her eyes the entire time, as if performing only for herself.

I didn't understand the words. I didn't need to.
