There is a ramen counter in Shibuya with eleven seats, no reservations, and a queue that forms before noon. I went once on a Tuesday and then again on a Thursday, which tells you something about how that first bowl landed.

## What Makes the Broth

The owner — a small, precise man who moves with the focused economy of someone who has made the same motions ten thousand times — starts his tonkotsu at four in the morning. By service time, the broth is opaque white, silky in a way that coats the back of the spoon. It doesn't taste like pork exactly. It tastes like warmth.

## The Egg Problem

Soft-boiled ramen eggs are one of those things that seem simple and are not. The window between a jammy yolk and a rubbery one is approximately ninety seconds. Get the marination wrong and they taste like nothing. Get the timing right and the protein chemistry right and they are an essential component of the bowl, adding richness and a sweet-salty depth that plays against the broth.

This place gets the egg right every time.
